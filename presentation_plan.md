# Advocacy & Presentation Plan: Retaining Full-Time SPED at Morningside Elementary

---

## Overview

This plan outlines a multi-pronged strategy to present the case for retaining full-time special education staffing at Morningside Elementary. Each audience requires different framing, evidence emphasis, and communication channels.

---

## Audience 1: District Leaders & Administration

### Key Decision-Makers
- Special Education Director (Bryce B Day)
- Elementary Special Education Associate Director (Tifny Iacona)
- Superintendent and Deputy Superintendents
- Budget/Finance Directors

### What They Care About
- Compliance risk and legal liability
- Budget efficiency (not just cost reduction)
- Data and metrics
- Avoiding due process complaints

### Core Arguments
1. **The district's own threshold is now exceeded.** Morningside has 21 K-4 students — above the 20-student minimum. The data has changed since the decision was made.
2. **Compliance risk is a budget risk.** A single due process complaint costs more than the salary difference between full-time and half-time. Failure to deliver IEP service minutes creates compensatory services liability.
3. **Workload, not caseload.** Present a workload analysis showing total IEP service minutes, meeting obligations, collaboration requirements, and paperwork demands for 21 students across K-4. Show that this cannot be compressed into half-time.
4. **Enrollment is not static.** New students transfer in, kindergartners with IEPs arrive in fall, and Child Find may identify additional students. A count taken at one point in time is already outdated.
5. **SB 241 makes this untenable.** Utah's new Early Literacy law (effective July 1, 2026 — the same school year) requires individualized reading plans, literacy team participation, benchmark coordination, and retention exemption documentation for SPED students. This substantially increases the special education teacher's workload at the exact moment the district proposes to cut their time in half.

### Deliverables
- [ ] **One-page executive brief** with the updated count (21 vs. threshold of 20), workload analysis summary, and compliance risk bullets
- [ ] **Workload calculation spreadsheet** showing total required IEP service minutes per week vs. available teacher minutes at half-time
- [ ] **Formal letter/memo** from concerned parents and/or the school's PTA requesting reconsideration based on new data
- [ ] **Meeting request** with the Special Education Director and Associate Director — present the data in person

### Tone
Professional, data-driven, solution-oriented. Frame it as: "The data has changed — the decision should be revisited."

## Audience 2: School Board Members

### What They Care About
- Community trust and constituent concerns
- Legal exposure for the district
- Equity and access
- Public perception

### Core Arguments
1. **Federal law requires FAPE regardless of staffing decisions.** Board members are ultimately responsible for ensuring the district meets its IDEA obligations. This is not discretionary.
2. **Granite SD already serves a higher-than-average SPED population (13% vs. 11% state average).** Cutting services in this context is moving in the wrong direction.
3. **Parent and community stories.** Real families, real children, real consequences. Board members respond to constituents.
4. **Penny wise, pound foolish.** The cost savings of half-time vs. full-time is minimal compared to the risk of due process complaints, compensatory services, and reputational damage.

### Deliverables
- [ ] **Public comment testimony** (3 minutes) — prepared script for board meeting, delivered by a parent and/or the school principal
- [ ] **Packet for board members** — executive brief + 2-3 parent stories + research summary (1-2 pages) + the workload calculation
- [ ] **Petition or sign-on letter** from Morningside families demonstrating breadth of concern
- [ ] **Attendance at board meeting** — visible community presence matters

### Tone
Respectful but urgent. Emphasize student impact and legal obligation. Avoid adversarial framing — position the board as allies who can fix this.

## Audience 3: General Public / Community

### What They Care About
- Children's wellbeing
- Fair treatment of vulnerable students
- Tax dollars being used wisely
- Accountability

### Core Arguments
1. **These are kids who need more help, not less.** Simple, human framing. Special education students have legal rights to services tailored to their needs.
2. **The school already qualifies for full-time by the district's own rules.** The threshold is 20; Morningside has 21. This is straightforward.
3. **A half-time teacher can't be in two places at once.** When a student has a crisis on a day the teacher is at the other school, who responds?
4. **National research shows this hurts students.** 72% of SPED teachers say large caseloads prevent them from meeting student needs. Students of burned-out teachers have worse academic outcomes.

### Deliverables
- [ ] **Social media campaign** — shareable graphics with key stats (see suggested content below)
- [ ] **Community information flyer** (1-page, plain language) for distribution at school events, PTA meetings, etc.
- [ ] **FAQ sheet** addressing likely counterarguments ("Isn't 21 students manageable?" "Can't a half-time teacher still meet needs?")
- [ ] **Online petition** (Change.org or similar) to demonstrate community support

### Suggested Social Media Content
- "Morningside Elementary has 21 special education students. The district's own threshold for a full-time teacher is 20. Why is the position being cut to half-time?"
- "72% of special education teachers say large caseloads prevent them from meeting student needs. Now Granite SD wants to split one teacher between two schools."
- "Federal law guarantees every child with a disability a Free Appropriate Public Education. A half-time teacher serving 21+ students puts that guarantee at risk."

### Tone
Accessible, emotional, factual. Lead with children, back up with data.

## Audience 4: Media

### What They Care About
- Conflict/tension (district vs. families)
- Human interest (individual student stories)
- Data that tells a clear story
- Systemic issues (teacher shortage, special education funding)

### Core Arguments
1. **The local angle on a national crisis.** Special education teacher shortages are a national story — Morningside is a compelling local example of a district making the problem worse by choice.
2. **The numbers don't add up.** The district set a threshold of 20. Morningside has 21. The decision was made when they had 19 — now the facts have changed but the decision hasn't.
3. **Parent stories.** Specific families willing to share their children's needs and their concerns (with appropriate privacy protections).
4. **Legal obligations at stake.** IDEA/FAPE is a strong hook — the district is legally required to provide these services.

### Target Outlets
- **Local TV news** (KSL, KUTV, KTVX, Fox 13) — human interest angle
- **Salt Lake Tribune** — education reporter, in-depth coverage
- **Deseret News** — education/family beat
- **Utah Education Network / education-focused outlets**
- **Local radio** (KSL Radio, KUER/NPR Utah)

### Deliverables
- [ ] **Press release** (1 page) — headline: district reducing special education staffing at school that exceeds its own threshold for full-time support
- [ ] **Media kit** — press release + fact sheet + parent quotes + research summary + contact information
- [ ] **2-3 families willing to speak on the record** (with coaching on key messages and privacy considerations)
- [ ] **Op-ed draft** for Salt Lake Tribune or Deseret News — authored by a parent, teacher, or community member

### Tone
Newsworthy, factual, compelling. Give reporters a clear narrative: the system is failing these kids and here's the proof.

## Cross-Cutting Strategy

### Timeline
1. **Week 1:** Finalize workload analysis spreadsheet and executive brief. Gather updated student count data. Identify parent spokespeople.
2. **Week 2:** Submit formal request to district leadership for meeting to present new data. Begin collecting parent signatures for petition/letter.
3. **Week 3:** If district does not respond or reverses, escalate: submit public comment for next board meeting, distribute media kit, launch social media campaign.
4. **Week 4+:** Attend board meeting with community presence. Follow up with media. File formal complaint with Utah State Board of Education if no resolution.

### Key Messages (Consistent Across All Audiences)
1. **Morningside now has 21 K-4 special education students — exceeding the district's own 20-student threshold for full-time staffing.**
2. **The decision was made using outdated data (19 students). The facts have changed; the decision should too.**
3. **Federal law (IDEA) requires a Free Appropriate Public Education. Staffing decisions cannot reduce legal obligations.**
4. **Research shows that overloaded special education teachers burn out, and their students suffer worse outcomes.**
5. **A half-time teacher cannot deliver the same quality of service as a full-time teacher for 21 students across 5 grade levels.**
6. **Utah's new SB 241 Early Literacy law takes effect this same school year, adding individualized reading plans, literacy teams, and retention processes to the special education teacher's workload. This is the worst possible time to cut staffing.**

### Potential Counterarguments & Responses

| Counterargument | Response |
|----------------|----------|
| "21 is close enough to 20 — it's manageable at half-time" | 20 is the threshold for *full-time*. Half-time means the teacher has roughly 50% of available hours — that's a workload designed for ~10 students, not 21. |
| "The teacher can manage IEPs from the other school remotely" | IEP services require in-person instruction. IEP meetings require team participation. Crisis response requires physical presence. Remote management is not a substitute. |
| "Budget constraints require shared staffing" | The cost difference between full-time and half-time is far less than the cost of a single due process complaint, which can run $50,000-$100,000+. This is a false economy. |
| "Other schools share teachers successfully" | Sharing works for low-incidence specialists serving 2-3 students per school. It does not work for a teacher serving as primary case manager for 21 students requiring daily services. |
| "Enrollment could drop" | Enrollment could also increase. Kindergarten transitions, mid-year transfers, and new evaluations all add students. Staffing decisions based on the lowest possible count guarantee inadequate service when enrollment is average or above. |
| "SB 241 is about general education, not special education" | SB 241 requires special education teachers to serve on literacy teams, help develop individualized reading plans alongside IEPs, coordinate benchmark accommodations, and participate in retention exemption decisions. It directly increases SPED teacher workload. |
