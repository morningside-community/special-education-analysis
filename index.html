Morningside Special Education Staffing: A Data Brief

A Research-Based Analysis of Special Education Staffing Needs at Morningside Elementary School, Granite School District

April 2026

The Situation

Morningside Elementary School in Granite School District currently serves 28 students with IEPs. Of these, 21 are in grades K–4 and will continue next year (7 fifth-graders will move on). Granite School District has set 20 students as the minimum threshold for justifying a full-time special education teacher. Despite now exceeding this threshold, the district plans to reduce Morningside's full-time special education position to half-time, with the teacher splitting their time between Morningside and a second school.

What Does "Full-Time Available" Mean?

A full-time teacher's contract is typically 37.5 hours/week (7.5 hrs/day × 5 days). Not all of that time is available for special education responsibilities:

Caseload Comparison

Morningside currently serves 28 students with IEPs. The projected count of 21 reflects only K–4 students continuing next year after 7 fifth-graders leave — and does not account for incoming kindergartners or mid-year transfers.

50% of new special education hires in Granite School District do not yet hold a special education license. [1] The district is already training new teachers — reducing positions further compounds the staffing challenge. Utah's own Special Education Advisory Panel recommends that adequate staffing requires "belonging within a school community and an appropriate caseload" [3] — both of which are undermined by split assignments. Statewide survey data confirms that working conditions are directly linked to special education teacher satisfaction and retention. [2]

SB 241: New Mandates, Less Staff

Utah Senate Bill 241 (Early Literacy), signed by the Governor on March 18, 2026 [4], creates sweeping new literacy requirements for K–3 students. The law takes effect July 1, 2026 — the same school year the district proposes to cut Morningside's special education position to half-time. [5]

Key Requirements
  • Individualized Reading Plans (IRPs) for all K–3 students scoring below benchmark
  • Literacy teams including special education teachers to develop and monitor IRPs
  • Benchmark reading assessments expanded to kindergarten
  • Third-grade retention for students not meeting reading benchmarks (with IEP exemptions)
  • Science of reading professional development required for K–3 teachers

Legal Obligations

The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) guarantees every eligible student a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE). [8] These obligations are not optional and do not decrease when staffing is reduced. [9], [10]

  • Staffing shortages do not reduce FAPE obligations. Districts must provide all IEP-specified services regardless of staffing levels. [9]
  • IEP teams cannot determine services based on staffing availability or cost. Services must be determined by student need. [8]
  • If a student's IEP requires a service the district does not offer, the district must ensure the student receives it — at district expense. [10]
  • States cannot waive special education personnel certification requirements on an emergency or provisional basis. [9]

Compliance Risks at Half-Time

  • Failure to deliver IEP service minutes
  • IEP meeting scheduling bottlenecks
  • Delayed progress monitoring
  • Reduced Child Find capacity
  • Compensatory services liability

Cost Comparison

Granite School District estimates the cost of a due process hearing at approximately $70,000 per case, based on attorney fees of $400/hour over approximately 175 hours. [1] The annual salary difference between a full-time and half-time position is substantially less than this amount.
