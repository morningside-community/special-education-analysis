Morningside Elementary: Special Education Staffing Data Brief

Executive Summary — Granite School District — May 2026

The Problem

Granite School District plans to reduce Morningside Elementary's full-time special education teacher to half-time, splitting the position between two schools. The decision was made when the K–4 special education count was 19 students. It is now 20 — meeting the district's own threshold of 20 for full-time staffing — and the school currently serves 28 students total including fifth-graders.

The Workload Gap

Published research shows each student requires 1.54–2.42 hours/week of teacher time (instruction, IEP management, paperwork, collaboration, parent communication, and SB 241 obligations). After morning duty, recess, lunch, staff meetings, and other responsibilities, a teacher has:

SB 241: New Mandates, Same Year

Utah SB 241 (Early Literacy), effective July 1, 2026, adds 74 hours/year of new responsibilities for the special education teacher: individualized reading plans, literacy team participation, benchmark coordination, retention exemption documentation, and required professional development — the same school year the position would be cut.

Obligations (IDEA/FAPE)

  • Staffing reductions do not reduce the obligation to deliver all IEP-specified services
  • IEP teams cannot determine services based on staffing availability or cost
  • Failure to deliver services creates compensatory services liability

Annual Cost-Risk Analysis

Reducing the position to half-time saves approximately $55,000/year (half of ~$110,000 total compensation). The potential costs from reduced service capacity include:

The $55,000 savings is exceeded by a single due process case ($70,000) or compensatory services order. The potential annual exposure substantially exceeds the savings.

What Has Changed

  • Student count increased from 19 to 20, now meeting the district's own threshold
  • SB 241 adds substantial new workload starting the same school year
  • Incoming kindergartners and mid-year transfers could push the count above 20
The data supports revisiting this decision. A workload-based analysis using conservative estimates and the district's own numbers demonstrates that half-time staffing for 20 students across five grade levels creates an unworkable gap.
