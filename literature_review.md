# Literature Review: Special Education Staffing, Caseload, and Student Outcomes

## Prepared in Support of Retaining Full-Time Special Education Staffing at Morningside Elementary School, Granite School District

---

## 1. Executive Summary

This literature review synthesizes current research on special education staffing decisions, caseload/workload analysis, teacher burnout and attrition, student outcomes, and federal legal obligations under IDEA. It also examines how Utah's newly signed SB 241 (Early Literacy, 2026) creates additional workload demands that make reducing special education staffing particularly ill-timed.

The evidence overwhelmingly supports the conclusion that reducing a full-time special education teacher to half-time at a school serving 21+ students with IEPs creates significant risks to:

- **Student outcomes** (academic achievement, IEP goal attainment, behavioral support)
- **Legal compliance** (FAPE obligations under IDEA)
- **Teacher retention** (burnout and attrition from unsustainable workloads)
- **Service delivery fidelity** (ability to deliver IEP-mandated minutes and services)
- **SB 241 implementation** (individualized reading plans, literacy team participation, benchmark monitoring)

---

## 2. Caseload vs. Workload: Why Raw Student Counts Are Insufficient

### 2.1 The Shift from Caseload to Workload Analysis

The National Education Association (NEA) advocates replacing traditional caseload structures with a **workload analysis model** that accounts for the full scope of a special educator's responsibilities — not just the number of students on their roster.[^1] A caseload number (e.g., "20 students") does not capture:

- **Direct instruction time** required by each student's IEP
- **IEP development, review, and revision** (annual IEPs, triennial re-evaluations, amendment meetings)
- **Consultation with general education teachers** across multiple grade levels (K-4)
- **Co-teaching and push-in services** in inclusive settings
- **Parent communication and collaboration**
- **Progress monitoring and data collection**
- **Compliance paperwork and documentation**
- **Unplanned crises and emergencies**

Research from NEA found that special educators are typically expected to devote nearly **6 additional hours weekly** beyond their available time to meet all responsibilities — even under current caseload conditions.[^1]

The Texas Association of School Boards (TASB) explains this distinction clearly: a caseload approach counts students without considering the intensity or variety of needs, while a workload approach considers the time and effort needed for each student's unique IEP requirements.[^2]

### 2.2 State Policy Variation

A 2020 peer-reviewed study published in the *Journal of Special Education Leadership* found that only 20 states have specific policies about caseload size, while 20 states have no guidance at all.[^3] Utah does not mandate specific caseload caps for special education teachers, meaning districts like Granite set their own thresholds. This makes local advocacy essential — there is no state-level backstop.

### 2.3 What a Workload Approach Reveals

When workload is analyzed properly using the NEA three-step process, a teacher serving 21 students across grades K-4 with varying disability categories is managing:

- **21+ IEPs** (each requiring annual review, progress reports each grading period, and triennial re-evaluations for a subset)
- **Multiple disability categories** with different instructional demands
- **5-6 grade-level teams** requiring separate consultation/collaboration time
- **21+ parent/family relationships** requiring ongoing communication
- **Differentiated instruction** across reading, math, writing, and behavior domains

The Illinois State Board of Education's workload plan guidance confirms that workload encompasses case management, academic and nonacademic instructional support for multiple content areas and disabilities, adapting or creating curricula, and collaborating across grade levels with teachers and other service providers.[^4]

Cutting this position to half-time means all of these responsibilities must be compressed into roughly **15-17.5 hours per week** of on-site availability.

## 3. Impact on Student Outcomes

### 3.1 Direct Effects of Teacher Availability

Research consistently shows that students in smaller instructional groups learn more, and that reduced student-teacher ratios result in more individualized instruction.[^5][^6] A 2025 study in *Behavior Analysis in Practice* found that higher teacher-student ratios correlated with increased student engagement and fewer behavioral interventions needed.[^7]

### 3.2 The Burnout-Outcomes Connection

A robust body of research (2020-2025) demonstrates that:

- **Students of teachers with high burnout experience lower academic outcomes** and less IEP goal attainment.[^8][^9]
- **72% of special education teachers** report that large caseloads negatively impact their ability to meet student needs.[^10]
- Special education teachers who teach **smaller groups with more homogeneous needs** report manageable workloads, less emotional exhaustion, and greater intent to stay in their positions.[^11]
- A 2024 study in the *European Journal of Special Needs Education* confirmed that excessive workload — particularly bureaucratic activities and excessive student numbers — is a primary contributor to burnout.[^12]

### 3.3 Achievement Gaps for Students with Disabilities

National data paints a stark picture:

- **72% of 4th graders with disabilities** scored below basic in reading on the 2024 NAEP
- **53% scored below basic in math** (2024 NAEP)
- Students with disabilities are performing **even worse** post-pandemic compared to general education peers
- Students with disabilities are more likely to be assigned to novice or less effective teachers[^13]

These gaps demand *more* intensive support, not less.

## 4. Teacher Burnout, Attrition, and the Staffing Crisis

### 4.1 The Special Education Teacher Shortage

- Nearly **half of all public schools** reported special education vacancies in 2022-24[^13]
- **21% of schools** reported at least one special education vacancy in 2022-23[^14]
- **55% of schools** reported difficulty filling special education positions[^14]
- Burnout is the **leading cause** of the special education teacher shortage[^11]
- **10% of all special educators** leave teaching within 6 years[^3]

### 4.2 What Drives Burnout

Research identifies the following primary burnout drivers, all of which are *exacerbated* by a half-time split assignment:

| Factor | Full-Time (1 school) | Half-Time (2 schools) |
|--------|---------------------|-----------------------|
| Caseload size | Manageable at 21 | 21 + second school's caseload |
| Planning time | Adequate | Severely reduced |
| Collaboration with gen-ed teachers | Daily availability | Limited to 2-3 days/week |
| Travel time | None | Lost instructional time |
| Relationship with students/families | Consistent | Fragmented |
| Administrative burden | Standard | Doubled (2 schools, 2 admin teams) |
| Crisis response availability | Immediate | Delayed or unavailable |

Special educators who rate their planning time as adequate are less likely to experience burnout and less likely to plan to leave their jobs. Emerging evidence indicates special educators may be more likely to use evidence-based practices and provide higher-quality instruction when they have adequate planning time.[^14]

### 4.3 Granite School District Context

Granite School District's special education population stands at approximately **13% of enrollment** — notably higher than the Utah state average of **11%**. The district serves a diverse population including a significant number of refugees and students from lower socioeconomic backgrounds. Behavioral challenges among students have been increasing, adding further strain to educators.[^15]

## 5. Legal Obligations Under IDEA and FAPE

### 5.1 Non-Negotiable Requirements

The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) guarantees every eligible student a **Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE)**.[^16] Key legal principles:

- **Staffing shortages do not reduce FAPE obligations.** Districts remain legally required to provide all services specified in each student's IEP regardless of staffing levels.[^17]
- **IEP teams cannot determine services based on staffing availability or cost.** Services must be determined by student need (IDEA, 34 CFR 300.320-300.324).[^16]
- **If a student's IEP requires a service the district doesn't offer, the district must ensure the student receives it** — at district expense if necessary.[^18]
- **States cannot waive special education personnel certification requirements** on an emergency or provisional basis (OSEP guidance).[^17]

### 5.2 Compliance Risks of Half-Time Staffing

With a half-time teacher serving 21 students across K-4, the following compliance risks emerge:

1. **Failure to deliver IEP service minutes**: If total mandated service minutes exceed what can be delivered in 2.5-3 days per week, the district is in violation of each affected student's IEP.
2. **IEP meeting scheduling**: Annual reviews, re-evaluations, and amendment meetings for 21 students must be scheduled within the teacher's limited on-site days, creating bottlenecks and potential timeline violations.
3. **Progress monitoring**: Less frequent data collection may result in inaccurate or late progress reports to parents.
4. **Child Find obligations**: With reduced presence, the teacher has less opportunity to identify students who may need evaluation — a core IDEA requirement.[^16]
5. **Compensatory services liability**: If the district fails to deliver IEP services, it may be required to provide compensatory education at significant additional cost.

### 5.3 Due Process Risk

Parents who believe their child is not receiving FAPE may file due process complaints or complaints with the Utah State Board of Education. The cost of a single due process hearing — including legal fees, expert witnesses, and potential compensatory services — can far exceed the cost difference between a full-time and half-time position.

## 6. Utah SB 241 (Early Literacy, 2026): New Demands on Special Education Teachers

### 6.1 What SB 241 Requires

Utah Senate Bill 241, signed by the Governor on March 18, 2026, with provisions taking effect July 1, 2026, creates sweeping new literacy mandates for K-3 students:[^19][^20][^21]

- **Individualized Reading Plans (IRPs)**: Schools must develop individualized reading plans for all K-3 students scoring "below" or "well below" benchmark on Acadience reading assessments.[^22]
- **Literacy Teams**: Each IRP must be developed by a "literacy team" that includes the student's teachers — both general education and special education — plus parents, literacy specialists, and support staff.[^23]
- **Benchmark testing expansion**: Acadience reading assessments are extended to kindergarten (previously grades 1-3).[^22]
- **Third-grade retention**: Beginning in the 2029-2030 school year, third graders not reading at grade level face mandatory retention, with limited exemptions.[^22]
- **Science of reading training**: K-3 teachers must complete professional development in the science of reading, and fluency in reading instruction is now part of annual performance evaluations.[^20][^22]
- **Accountability**: Schools below the 80% third-grade proficiency goal (target: July 1, 2030) must redirect at least 50% of Teacher and Student Success Program funding toward literacy interventions.[^20]

### 6.2 How SB 241 Directly Increases Special Education Teacher Workload

For special education teachers at elementary schools like Morningside, SB 241 creates **substantial new responsibilities on top of existing IEP obligations**:

| New SB 241 Responsibility | Impact on SPED Teacher |
|---------------------------|----------------------|
| Literacy team member for each K-3 SPED student with reading deficiency | Additional meetings, planning, and collaboration for potentially all 21 students |
| Individualized Reading Plan development | A new individualized plan *in addition to* the IEP — separate documentation, separate goals, separate progress monitoring |
| Benchmark assessment coordination | Ensuring SPED students are appropriately assessed; coordinating accommodations and determining whether benchmark testing is appropriate per IEP |
| Science of reading professional development | Required training hours competing with already-limited time |
| Retention decision participation | For 3rd graders with IEPs, the SPED teacher must participate in determining whether the "good cause" disability exemption applies |
| Parent communication about IRPs | Additional parent conferences beyond IEP meetings — families must be "in those conversations with the school"[^24] |

### 6.3 The IRP + IEP Collision

Students with disabilities who also have reading deficiencies will now have **two individualized plans** managed simultaneously:

- An **IEP** (required by IDEA) with reading goals, service minutes, accommodations, and progress monitoring
- An **Individualized Reading Plan** (required by SB 241) with its own interventions, benchmarks, and literacy team oversight

The special education teacher must ensure these plans are **aligned and not contradictory**, participate in both sets of meetings, and manage progress monitoring for both. For a teacher serving 21 students K-4 — many of whom are likely reading below benchmark given that 72% of 4th graders with disabilities nationally score below basic in reading — this effectively doubles the planning and documentation workload for a significant portion of the caseload.

### 6.4 The Retention Exemption Process Adds Work

SB 241 exempts students with disabilities from mandatory third-grade retention only where "benchmark testing isn't appropriate" per the student's IEP or 504 plan.[^22] This means:

- The SPED teacher must **document and justify** why benchmark testing is or isn't appropriate for each 3rd grader with an IEP
- If a 3rd grader with an IEP *is* subject to retention, the SPED teacher participates in the **retention decision and appeal process**
- Research shows that among children retained in third grade, approximately **18% had an IEP** at the time of retention, and an additional 10% received an IEP in the 1-2 years following retention.[^25] Retained students with IEPs require intensive ongoing support.

### 6.5 Timing Could Not Be Worse

SB 241 takes effect **July 1, 2026** — the same school year Granite SD proposes to cut Morningside's SPED teacher to half-time. The district is simultaneously:

- **Adding** major new literacy mandates that directly increase special education teacher workload
- **Reducing** the staffing capacity to carry out those mandates

This creates an impossible situation. A half-time teacher cannot fulfill existing IEP obligations for 21 students *and* take on SB 241's individualized reading plan requirements, literacy team participation, benchmark coordination, and retention exemption documentation.

## 7. The Itinerant/Split-Assignment Model: What Research Shows

### 7.1 Challenges of the Split Model

While itinerant special education models (teachers serving multiple schools) exist and can work in some contexts, research highlights critical challenges:[^26]

- **Reduced collaboration time** with general education teachers at each site
- **Loss of instructional time to travel** between buildings
- **Difficulty building relationships** with students, families, and staff
- **Fragmented crisis response** — the teacher is unavailable when incidents occur at the other school
- **Dual administrative demands** — two sets of meetings, two school cultures, two leadership teams

### 7.2 When Itinerant Models Work vs. When They Don't

Itinerant models are most effective for **low-incidence** specialists (e.g., teachers of students who are deaf/hard of hearing, visually impaired) where a single school may have only 1-3 students requiring that expertise. They are **least appropriate** when:

- The caseload at a single school is large enough to fill a full-time position (as at Morningside with 21 students)
- Students require **daily** specialized instruction (as most IEPs mandate)
- The teacher serves as the **primary case manager** for IEPs (as opposed to a consulting role)

## 8. The Numbers at Morningside: A Closer Look

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Current SPED enrollment | 28 |
| K-4 students (continuing) | 21 |
| 5th graders departing | 7 |
| Granite SD threshold for full-time | 20 |
| Current K-4 count vs. threshold | **21 (exceeds by 1)** |
| Count when decision was made | 19 |
| Count now | 21 |

**Critical points:**
- The district's own threshold of 20 is already exceeded
- The count of 19 used to justify the decision is no longer accurate
- New enrollments and transfers during the school year could increase the number further — special education enrollment is not static
- Incoming kindergartners with IEPs (preschool-to-K transitions) will add to the caseload in fall
- The "20 student" threshold is a caseload number that does not account for workload — as the research above demonstrates, workload is the more appropriate measure
- SB 241 adds workload that did not exist when the 20-student threshold was set
